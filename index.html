About Me

Hi! I'm an incoming Master's student in Computational Social Science at the University of Chicago, excited to explore how we can leverage artificial intelligence—particularly language models—to better understand and address complex social phenomena.

I'm particularly interested in designing AI systems that not only solve practical problems but also highlight diverse viewpoints and biases. I believe the future of AI lies in its ability to integrate various disciplinary perspectives, enabling us to interpret social phenomena more accurately and communicate more effectively.

Before joining UChicago, I earned my bachelor's degrees from UC San Diego, majoring in Applied Mathematics and Cognitive Science (with a specialization in Machine Learning and Neural Computation) and minoring in History.

Research Interests

I'm particularly excited about exploring domain-specific text complexity adaptation. I'm interested in understanding how AI can better recognize and handle the unique textual variations across different disciplines like history, literature, and social sciences.

I'm also drawn to the idea of using AI and language models to bridge methodological divides between different research traditions. I believe the way scientists frame their research profoundly influences their methods and solutions, and I'm curious about how computational tools might facilitate better communication and understanding between natural and social sciences.

Publications

Beyond LLMs: A Linguistic Approach to Causal Graph Generation from Narrative Texts

Zehan Li, Ruhua Pan, Xinyu Pi

Proceedings of the The 7th Workshop on Narrative Understanding, NAACL , 2025

We propose a novel framework to generate causal graphs from narrative texts, bridging high-level causality and event-specific relationships. Our approach extracts agent-centered "vertices" using LLM-based summarization, then applies linguistically grounded features through a STAC classification model. This hybrid system achieves superior precision in identifying causal links compared to LLM-only baselines, consistently outperforming GPT-4o and Claude 3.5 across key dimensions of causal graph quality.
